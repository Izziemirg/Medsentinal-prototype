Expert Interview: Specialty Pharmacist Perspective on ADR Surveillance

A semi-structured interview was conducted to understand real-world ADR reporting workflows, pain points with existing tools, and expert perspective on the MedSentinel concept. Key findings directly inform the design of the triage dashboard, severity model, and AI copilot features.

🎙️
Interviewer
Devika Jaipur
Product Design Manager · Module 4, Team 9
👨‍⚕️
Interviewee
Dr. Bruce Jaipur, PharmD
Specialty Pharmacist · Clinical Setting
🎯
Research Focus
ADR Reporting Workflow
Pain points · Tool validation · AI readiness

📋 Interview Overview & Summary

Dr. Jaipur currently uses two reporting systems: MedWatch for formal FDA submissions and an internal EHR system for less severe cases. He reports directly to the FDA, after which submissions are distributed to other stakeholders — however, he has never received any feedback or follow-up from the FDA after submitting. MedWatch is described as well-established but lengthy and cumbersome, with the primary barrier being the time required to complete submissions.

A major pain point is duplication of effort — ADR information must first be entered into the internal EHR system and then manually re-entered into MedWatch. This burden, combined with general busyness in clinical settings, means only the most severe events typically get formally reported — leaving a significant gap in surveillance data.

Severity assessment is informal and judgment-based, relying primarily on clinical knowledge with Lexicomp consulted for knowledge gaps. Patient-reported sources like social media are taken "with a grain of salt" — acknowledged as having some value, but lacking full clinical context. On AI tools, Dr. Jaipur sees potential in real-time monitoring that could flag overlooked ADRs based on patient chart data, but emphasized that managing alert fatigue would be critical to its usefulness.

⚠️ Critical Pain Point
Under-reporting due to workload
Only severe events get formally reported. Hospitals and pharmacies are too busy for routine submissions.
🔁 Workflow Friction
Double data entry required
ADR data entered into internal EHR must be manually re-entered into MedWatch — no integration exists.
🤖 AI Opportunity
Real-time chart monitoring
AI could flag ADRs missed during busy periods by monitoring patient charts — but must avoid alert fatigue.

🗣️ Notable Quotes

— On AI-assisted surveillance

— On Voice of Customer (VoC) data

💬 Interview Questions & Responses

Q1 · ADR Submission Workflow
How do you currently submit an ADR? Do you use multiple systems? Which do you prefer?
Dr. Jaipur uses two systems: MedWatch for formal FDA submissions and an internal organizational system for less severe cases. He reports directly to the FDA, after which reports are distributed to other stakeholders. He describes MedWatch as well-established, but notes the submission process is long and cumbersome — the biggest barrier is finding the time to complete and submit a report, not the form itself.
Q2 · MedWatch Challenges
What challenges do you have when interfacing with MedWatch, if any?
The form itself is well-established and not confusing — the main challenge is duplication of effort. ADR information is first entered into the organization's internal EHR (electronic medical record) system, and then must be manually re-entered into MedWatch separately. There is no integration between the two systems. This duplication, combined with heavy clinical workloads, means many ADR reports never make it to the FDA at all.
Q3 · FDA Feedback Loop
Does he feel the FDA processes submissions in a reasonable amount of time?
Dr. Jaipur has never received any feedback or follow-up from the FDA after submitting a report. There is no response loop — submissions go in and there is no acknowledgment or outcome communicated back to the reporter. This lack of feedback reduces motivation to report and makes it difficult to understand whether submissions are being acted upon.
Q4 · Voice of Customer (VoC) Data
Do you currently consider patient-reported sources (social media, forums) as valid signal inputs? Why or why not?
VoC data is taken "with a grain of salt." Dr. Jaipur acknowledges some validity in seeing what patients are self-reporting, but emphasizes that full clinical context is absent — a social media post doesn't include the patient's full medical history, comorbidities, or concomitant medications. This lack of context makes it difficult to attribute the reported symptom definitively to a specific drug. He currently collects ADR information firsthand from patients or hospitals and manually translates it into medical terminology, with no systematic VoC framework in place.
Q5 · Severity Assessment
How do you currently assess the severity or urgency of a potential ADR signal?
Severity assessment is primarily mental and non-systematic — relying on clinical knowledge and judgment developed through training and experience. There is no consistent or streamlined process. When knowledge gaps arise, Dr. Jaipur consults the Lexicomp database for drug interaction and adverse effect information. This informal approach means severity determinations may vary and are not reproducible in the way a calibrated scoring model would be.
Q6 · AI Tool Readiness
How would an AI-powered severity triage tool change your workflow? What would you need to trust it?
Dr. Jaipur sees clear value in a tool that could monitor patient chart inputs in real time and alert him to ADRs he might otherwise miss during a busy shift. The AI could surface connections between a patient's current medications and reported symptoms that a human might overlook. The key requirement for trust: the tool must effectively manage alert fatigue — if it flags too many false positives or low-severity events, clinicians will begin ignoring alerts entirely, defeating the purpose. The tool's value is tied directly to the precision of its prioritization.
Q7 · Biggest Pain Point
If you could change one thing about ADR surveillance, what would it be?
Two issues stood out. First, the nausea/vomiting/diarrhea problem: these symptoms appear as listed ADRs for nearly every drug, making it nearly impossible to identify which medications genuinely cause them versus those reported incidentally during trials. Second, the systemic under-reporting problem: because hospitals and pharmacies are overwhelmed, only the most severe events get formally reported. The majority of ADR events — particularly moderate-severity ones — never reach the FDA. A system that reduces the friction of reporting (or automates it) could dramatically improve surveillance coverage.

💡 Key Findings & MedSentinel Design Implications

📋
Auto-populated Report Builder (C5)
Double data entry pain point directly validates the Report Builder concept — AI auto-filling the MedWatch form from EHR data eliminates the duplication.
⚡
Severity Model Calibration (Architecture)
The informal, judgment-based severity process confirms the need for a calibrated 0–1 score with confidence intervals — giving reviewers a reproducible, auditable signal.
🤖
AI Copilot Alert Fatigue Design (C4)
Alert fatigue is the #1 risk for AI adoption. The Copilot must surface only high-confidence, high-severity escalations and give clear rationale — not a flood of flags.
📡
VoC as Supplemental Signal (C3)
Skepticism about social media data validates our approach: VoC is shown alongside formal FAERS/MedWatch data as corroborating evidence, not as the primary source.
